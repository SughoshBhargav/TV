A NOTE ON METHODOLOGY AND DATA HONESTY (please read before the report)

I searched extensively across manufacturer pages, Amazon.in, Flipkart, Reliance Digital, Smartprix, RTINGS, HDTVTest, FlatpanelsHD, AVForums, AVSForum, HiFiVision (India's main AV enthusiast forum), Trustpilot, and consumer-court records. Here is the honest state of the evidence:

  • Neither TV has been lab-tested by RTINGS, HDTVTest, FlatpanelsHD, WhatHiFi, or Tom's Guide. Those outlets review TCL's global flagship line (X11L, C8K/QM8K, QM9K, C855, C845) and Philips' European Ambilight/OLED range — not India-only budget/mid-range SKUs like the T89D or 75MLED610/94. I checked directly; there is no match.

  • That means specifics like measured contrast ratio, exact dimming-zone count, measured HDR peak nits, Delta E color accuracy, input lag in milliseconds, and panel manufacturer are not independently verified for these specific models. I am not going to invent numbers dressed up as lab data — I mark these "Data not available (no independent lab review found)" throughout, and instead tell you what is actually knowable: manufacturer-claimed specs, real user reports, pricing history, brand-level reliability patterns, and after-sales track record.

  • Where I do cite a number, it's either (a) directly from the official spec sheet / retailer listing, or (b) from a verified owner review, and I say so.

  • This is a genuinely close, budget-to-mid-range 75" QD-Mini LED matchup (~₹80,000–₹90,000 street price), not a flagship comparison — treat any source claiming lab-verified 5,000-nit brightness or "1,000+ dimming zones" for these exact SKUs with skepticism unless it cites a primary source.

SECTION 9 — LONG-TERM RELIABILITY

This is the section where "Data not available" is most honest, because 2026 models by definition cannot yet have 5–10 years of ownership data. No one has owned either TV for more than a few months. Anyone giving you a confident "average lifespan" or "failure rate" figure for the T89D or 75MLED610/94 specifically is guessing. What I can give you is real, sourced pattern data from each brand's broader India track record:

TCL (brand-level, India):

  • A verified 2025 HiFiVision forum thread documents a customer who received a DOA (dead-on-arrival) faulty panel on a TCL 75Q6C Mini-LED TV purchased via Amazon during a sale event, and then experienced a multi-week (16+ day, extending toward 20-25 day) replacement delay, with TCL's own "escalation department" unable to give a firm timeline, and Amazon eventually stepping in to offer a refund after the brand failed to resolve it.

  • A separate HiFiVision thread describes a panel failure during the warranty period where the technician falsely claimed the replacement panel was unavailable (despite it being available in the local market), requiring a legal notice to resolve.

  • These are anecdotal but verifiable, first-hand, dated reports — not a statistically representative failure rate, but a real pattern of slow/unreliable escalation and replacement-part logistics when things do go wrong, especially for large-panel Mini-LED sets sold through online marketplaces during high-volume sale periods.

Philips (brand-level, India — via SPPL/Skyworth license):

  • A January 2026 consumer court ruling (Ernakulam District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission) found Philips India and its retail partner liable for denying warranty/extended-warranty service on a Philips LED TV, awarding the customer repair-or-refund plus ₹15,000 compensation for "deliberate, arbitrary and unfair" conduct. This is a formal legal finding, not a forum anecdote, and it post-dates the SPPL/Skyworth relaunch context.

  • Multiple older but consistent forum/consumer-site threads (HiFiVision, MouthShut, JustDial, AVForums, Trustpilot) describe a long-standing pattern of poor after-sales responsiveness under the Philips brand in India across multiple TV licensees over the past decade — this pattern predates the current SPPL license but is worth knowing given it's the same brand name (even though the manufacturing entity keeps changing hands: Videocon → TPV → SPPL/Skyworth).

  • Because the current SPPL-manufactured Philips India TVs are new to market, there isn't yet enough post-2025 owner data specific to the SPPL era to say whether this pattern continues — this is a genuine unknown, not a settled verdict against the current product line.

Common failure categories to watch for on any large-panel Mini-LED TV in India (industry-general, not brand-specific): voltage-related power supply stress (India's less-regulated grid voltage is a widely-cited stress factor across all TV brands per multiple forum threads), backlight/zone failures, and Wi-Fi/software bugs on Google TV (session logouts, app crashes) — the T89D's own Flipkart reviews (Section 11) already show a live example of the Google-account-logout software bug.

Bottom line: you cannot get a real 5–10-year reliability verdict on a model that's been on sale for a few months. What you can rely on is that both brands have documented, verifiable histories of slow or adversarial after-sales service in India when something does go wrong — which should inform how much you value the extended warranty (see Section 19) rather than which specific TV you pick.

SECTION 19 — FINAL VERDICT

Which would I buy?

Genuinely, this is close, and I want to be honest that I can't give you a lab-backed "TV A wins on picture quality" answer because that data doesn't exist for either set. Weighing confirmed specs, real owner sentiment, and after-sales risk together, I'd lean toward the Philips 75MLED610/94 — mainly because of its lower price, its explicit HDR10+ support alongside Dolby Vision (broader real-world content compatibility), and its Apple AirPlay/HomeKit integration, which are all confirmed advantages rather than marketing claims. I would not call this a confident or emphatic recommendation — the port-count limitation (3 HDMI/1 USB) and the documented consumer-court finding against Philips India's warranty conduct are real, non-trivial downsides that could matter more to you than they do to a "typical" buyer.

Who should buy the TCL T89D?

  • Gamers who want the highest native refresh rate available in this segment and value a named, credible audio brand (Onkyo) over Apple-device integration.

  • Buyers who prioritize being able to walk into a Croma/Vijay Sales/Reliance Digital and physically test the set before buying, given TCL's broader offline retail footprint.

  • Households that don't use Apple devices and don't need AirPlay/HomeKit.

Who should buy the Philips 75MLED610/94?

  • Apple-ecosystem households (iPhone/Mac users who want native AirPlay/HomeKit).

  • Buyers who prioritize dual dynamic-HDR format support (Dolby Vision + HDR10+) for maximum streaming-service compatibility.

  • Budget-conscious buyers for whom the ~₹8,000–10,000 price gap matters, and who don't run more than 2–3 HDMI devices simultaneously.

Is the price difference justified?

Marginally, and only if you specifically value the TCL's higher refresh rate and named audio brand. On a pure feature-confirmed-per-rupee basis, the Philips currently looks like better value.

Would I wait for a sale?

Yes. Both listed MRPs (₹1,79,990 for TCL, ₹1,89,999 for Philips) are already being discounted by more than 50% to reach current street prices, which tells you the "real" market-clearing price is well below MRP — and India's major sale calendar events (festive-season sales, Republic Day sales, Amazon/Flipkart anniversary sales) routinely push prices on 75" sets in this segment down further, sometimes by another 5–10% beyond the "everyday" discounted price. Buying outside a sale window on either model is very likely leaving money on the table.

Would I recommend an extended warranty?

Yes, on either TV, and I'd treat it as close to non-optional. The documented after-sales patterns in Sections 9–10 — a real, dated case of TCL taking 16+ days (extending toward 20-25) to resolve a DOA panel, and a real, legally-adjudicated case of Philips India denying warranty service outright — are exactly the scenario an extended warranty (ideally purchased through the marketplace, e.g., Amazon's own protection plan, rather than relying solely on the OEM) is designed to protect against. A large-panel Mini-LED TV is precisely the kind of product where a backlight or panel-driver fault 14–20 months in is a realistic, non-remote risk.

Expected lifespan of each

Data not available with any specificity — these are 2026-model TVs with no field-failure history yet. Industry-typical LCD/Mini-LED backlight half-life estimates (an informal but widely used engineering benchmark) suggest usable performance for 5+ years of normal daily use is a reasonable expectation for a properly ventilated, stable-voltage installation, but this is a general LCD-category expectation, not a brand-specific guarantee for either model.

Expected maintenance costs

Beyond the 1-year included warranty, expect to budget for either an extended warranty (category-typical ₹3,000–₹7,000 for a 75" set, though not confirmed for these exact SKUs) or, if self-insuring, a realistic possibility of a ₹15,000–₹40,000+ out-of-warranty panel or backlight repair should something fail after year one — this range reflects general large-panel LCD/Mini-LED repair-cost patterns in India, not a specific quote for either model.

Potential risks

  1. Buying based on unverified marketing specs (nits, dimming zones, contrast) that no independent outlet has confirmed for either model.

  2. After-sales friction documented for both brands, with Philips having the more serious (legally adjudicated) failure mode on record.

  3. Voltage-related stress on large-panel electronics, a commonly cited factor across brands in India regardless of TV brand.

  4. Software-level bugs (confirmed for TCL: account logout) that are annoying but not catastrophic.

  5. For Philips specifically: the ongoing brand-licensee transition (Videocon → TPV → SPPL/Skyworth since Jan 2025) means the current product line has a shorter institutional track record than TCL's more continuous India operation.
